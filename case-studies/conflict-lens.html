The Problem

Understanding a conflict shouldn't take 12 browser tabs

When a conflict breaks, a single article gives you fragments, not understanding. The location is buried in one paragraph, the actors in another. The historical context is assumed. And the article's own slant is invisible unless you already know the outlet.

So an engaged reader does the work by hand: open a map to find where Khan Yunis actually is, skim Wikipedia for who's involved, cross-check a second outlet to sense the bias. Three tools, ten minutes, and most people give up before they start. Worse, the most rigorous reporting sits behind paywalls — so the readers who want depth hit a login wall first.

How might we take someone from a single article to a contextual, geographically-grounded, bias-aware understanding of a conflict — in under a minute?

Research & Insight

I started with the workflow, not the interface

Who it's for

The core user isn't a headline-skimmer — it's the engaged reader: students, journalists, analysts, globally-curious people who want to understand an event, not just hear it happened. Their frustration isn't a lack of news; it's a lack of synthesis. The information already exists — assembling it is the labor.

The market gap

Every existing tool solves one slice of the problem. AI summarizers compress text but strip out geography and offer no source skepticism. Live conflict maps nail geography but give you no synthesis, history, or bias. Traditional news has depth but no structure, no map, and one outlet's framing. The whitespace was clear: nobody combined synthesis, geography, and bias-awareness in a single view.

From insight to principles

The research converged on four principles that ended up driving every later decision:

01

Geography is the spine

The map leads; text supports. Location is the anchor everything else hangs from.

02

Never make them wait

Show the core fast, then stream the depth in behind it.

03

Make bias visible

Source framing is a feature you read, not a trap you fall into.

04

Meet readers where content is

Including when the best reporting sits behind a paywall.

Defining the Product

One flow, three moves

I kept the scope deliberately tight. No accounts, no saved history, no feeds — those would only dilute the one thing that had to feel like magic: the moment a wall of article text becomes a living briefing. The whole product is a single flow: Input → AI analysis → interactive briefing.

Diagram — 3-step flow [ Input: Link / PDF ] → [ Analyze ] → [ Interactive Briefing ] flow-3-step.png

Cut on purpose: no accounts · no saved history · no multi-article comparison. Defending what I left out mattered as much as choosing what to build.

The Interface — Desktop

Three screens, one continuous narrative

1 · Input

A quiet, editorial entry point — warm neutrals, a sage accent, a serif headline over clean sans-serif body. Restraint is the trust signal here: the product handles serious subject matter, so it should feel like it. A two-tab switcher offers Paste Link or Upload PDF (that second path exists for a specific reason — more in Decisions).

2 · Analysis

Instead of a generic spinner, the wait uses honest copy: "Extracting core incident… georeferencing primary location and key actors (~5–10s)." Truthful waiting copy lowers the perceived wait and signals that real work is happening, not a frozen screen.

3 · The Briefing Dashboard

A black-and-white satellite map fills the screen with translucent panels floating over it. On load, the map fits to every pin at the tightest zoom that still shows them all. Pins are color-coded — incident site, actor bases, conflict areas, timeline events — each radiating a soft colored ring. Three panel zones do the synthesis: Key Details (actors, location, date), The Briefing (a plain-language summary plus a journalistic-analysis panel on framing and source lean), and a Timeline of lead-up events — click one and the map flies to it. Everything loads progressively: the core paints first, then locations, timeline, and bias stream in behind it, each with its own inline loading cue.

Mobile — A Different Metaphor

The desktop pattern breaks on a phone — so I changed the metaphor

"Floating panels over a fullscreen map" is elegant on a desktop and unusable on a phone — the panels would either cover the map or overflow it. So mobile borrows the map-app pattern instead: the map is the hero and stays fully pannable, a compact top bar holds the title and a fresh-analysis button, and a bottom nav splits into Key Details · Summary · Timeline. Tapping one slides a sheet up over the map; an X or a tap on the map dismisses it. Tapping a pin auto-opens the Timeline, selects that event, and lifts the map so the pin isn't hidden behind the sheet.

Key Design Decisions

Decisions, and the tradeoffs behind them

Progressive rendering over one big result

TensionOne AI call is simpler to build, but means 20+ seconds of blank screen.

DecisionSplit it into stages — the core paints in ~5–10s, the rest streams in.

↳ Principle: Never make them wait

A PDF escape hatch for paywalls

TensionThe best reporting is paywalled, and a scraper just hits a login wall.

DecisionA second input mode — upload the article as a PDF. A product answer to a real-world constraint.

↳ Principle: Meet the reader where the content is

Bias as a visible panel

DecisionA dedicated journalistic-analysis panel: framing, institutional lean, missing cross-border context, and links to cross-reference.

↳ Principle: Make bias visible

Map-first, fit-to-pins

DecisionA full-bleed map that, on load, frames every pin at the tightest fitting zoom.

↳ Principle: Geography is the spine

A restrained, muted palette

DecisionOne sage accent family for all UI chrome; four muted pin colors are the only place hue carries meaning — so the map reads instantly.

Designing for Unreliable AI ★

The hardest design problem wasn't the UI — it was the AI

When your data source is a language model, it can return something wrong, malformed, or missing at any moment. Most of the real design work went into absorbing that gracefully — turning a probabilistic, sometimes-wrong source into an experience that still feels trustworthy.

Hallucinated source links

The model happily invented article URLs that 404'd. Instead of trusting them, I had it return an outlet plus a search query, then constructed guaranteed-valid search links myself — real, relevant, and never broken.

Coordinates that landed in the ocean

The model often returned 0.0, 0.0. The prompts now insist on real coordinates with capital-city fallbacks, and the UI filters out invalid pins and de-duplicates overlapping ones before anything reaches the map.

Failures that looked like empty states

A failed analysis used to render as a bare "No data." Now it shows a clear error with a one-tap Retry that re-runs only the step that failed — not the whole pipeline.

Rate limits mid-demo

I made the model configurable, moved to a tier with a separate quota, and stopped retrying on quota errors (which only burned through more of it).

Good UX for AI is as much about handling failure as showcasing success.

Concept → Build

What the build taught me that the concept didn't

"One AI call" → a four-stage pipeline

The single mega-call was slow and kept returning malformed JSON. I rebuilt it as scrape-once plus progressive core / locations / deep passes.

"Paste any URL" → URLs and PDFs

Real reporting is paywalled. The PDF mode exists entirely because my first assumption broke on contact with the real world.

"The AI returns clean map data" → defensive design

0.0, 0.0 coordinates and invented URLs forced me to engineer the whole experience around messy output rather than assuming clean input.

The concept was "paste a link, get a summary." The product became a resilient, progressively-rendered briefing engine — because real articles are paywalled, real AI output is messy, and real APIs rate-limit you.

The Stack — How It Works

Under the hood

Frontend
React + Vite, React-Leaflet (black-and-white satellite tiles), hand-built CSS theming.
Backend
Node + Express, with Google Gemini as the analysis engine.
Pipeline
Cheerio + Axios scrape the article; pdf-parse handles uploads; a short-lived session cache avoids re-scraping across the multi-step analysis; every AI call has retry / back-off.
Shipped
Frontend on GitHub Pages, backend on a free server tier.

Reflection & What's Next

What I learned, and where it goes

The biggest lesson was about designing for systems you don't fully control — progressive loading, honest waiting states, sensible fallbacks, and one-tap recovery. These aren't features users notice when they work; they're the difference between a demo and something you'd actually trust.

If I took it further: source-reliability scoring on the verification links, a "play through the timeline" walkthrough that flies the map from event to event in one click, saved briefings for analysts, and keyboard navigation through events with the map following along. The honest limitation I'd fix first: the model still occasionally returns events in mixed date formats, so a strict chronological guarantee and relative dates are the next thing on the list.
