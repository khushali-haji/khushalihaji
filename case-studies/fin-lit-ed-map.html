02 / The problem

One size fits all keeps failing

Most apps give everyone the same lessons. Three failures follow, and targeting fixes each.

Generic content

The same lessons for everyone, ignoring income, habits, and goals.

User fatigue

Too much complexity. People tune out and decide worse.

Context gap

Lessons ignore where someone lives and what it costs.

05 / The appetite

Nonwhite households want it more

Each person falls into one of three states: financial education was offered and they took it, it was offered and they skipped it, or it was never offered. Nonwhite households have a bigger took it block and a smaller never offered block. They engage more and are shut out less. Shown for 2024; the pattern holds across 2012 to 2024.

Took it Offered, skipped Never offered

Nonwhite households are offered financial education more often, and take it more. The one place white households lead is conversion among those already offered, near 74% versus 66%. But far fewer white households are offered it at all, so the appetite still runs higher among minority communities. That is the audience a targeted platform should serve.

Each state is a 3D block. Color is financial vulnerability (darker is worse, averaging five hardships: bills, emergency fund, overspending, overdrafts, predatory borrowing). Height is average income (taller is higher). A tall dark block means high income but high strain; a short dark block is the poorer, struggling pattern. 07 / Where this goes next

Beyond broad groups

The race groups here are coarse. One "Hispanic" or "Asian" number hides very different communities. The next cuts:

  • Immigration and generation. Recency of arrival shapes money habits more than race.
  • Country of origin. Mexican versus Cuban, Indian versus Vietnamese, very different lives.
  • Bigger samples. So small groups stay stable.

The NFCS and SCF do not record where someone was born. This would need other sources, the Census ACS, SIPP, or the Fed's SHED, as a separate module.
